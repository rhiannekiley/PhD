About me

Psychology researcher
in cancer communication

I'm a PhD researcher in the Department of Psychology at the University of Sheffield, jointly funded by the University of Sheffield and Oracle Cancer Trust. My research sits at the intersection of health communication, psychology, and cancer care — examining how the language we use to describe head and neck cancers shapes how they are understood and experienced.

Before starting my PhD, I completed an MSc in Applied Psychology, with a dissertation focused on patient experiences of HPV diagnosis within the UK cervical screening programme. I also volunteer with a biotech company working on HPV self-sampling technology.

I chose head and neck cancers specifically because communication in this field is critically understudied — particularly given the rapid rise of HPV-associated oropharyngeal cancer, which brings unique challenges around sexual health disclosure, stigma, and public awareness.

I'm committed to patient and public involvement as genuine co-production. My PPI group includes people with lived experience of HPV-related head and neck cancers, who actively shape research questions, methods, and interpretations throughout the project.

Research

Five studies across
two work packages

A mixed-methods programme examining how language shapes HNC communication across public, patient, and professional contexts.

Work Package 1 Public Awareness & Understanding
01
Social Media Discourse Around Celebrity HNC Cases
Reflexive thematic analysis of 1,797 Reddit items across five celebrity cases — examining how HPV disclosure type shapes public discourse, terminology use, and stigma.
Analysis underway
03
UK Public Knowledge Survey
A representative survey of ~1,500 UK adults via Prolific, exploring understanding of HNC terminology, risk factors, and HPV awareness using validated scales.
Planned
Work Package 2 Patient Experience
02
Healthcare Professional Communication Survey
Cross-sectional survey of ~220 UK ENTs, GPs, dentists, and primary care nurses — exploring knowledge gaps and HPV communication challenges in practice.
Data collection live
04
Patient Experience Interviews
In-depth qualitative interviews with ~15 HNC patients, exploring how diagnosis terminology affects their cancer journey, help-seeking, and access to support.
Planned
05
Family Member Experience Interviews
Parallel qualitative interviews with ~15 family members — investigating experiences navigating HNC terminology and supporting loved ones through HPV-related stigma.
Planned
